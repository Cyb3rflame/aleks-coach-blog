How we train

The way I think about it.

No long lists of fixes. The fault that matters, fixed properly, then the next one.

One change at a time

You can rebuild one thing well, not five at once. I pick the fault that matters most. Nothing else gets touched until it holds. Overwhelm is how progress stalls.

Fix the cause, not the symptom

A ball flying long is rarely a wrist problem. I trace it back through the kinetic chain to where it actually starts. Treat the cause and the symptom takes care of itself.

It feels weird before it works

Every real change feels wrong at first. You will shank a few. That is the change taking hold, not failing. I tell you up front so you push through the awkward phase instead of quitting on it.

Who you'd work with

A quick word on me.

I am LTA qualified and I have played national tournaments. I coach mechanics and performance, and I have worked with national competitors. But the part I care about is the one most coaches skip. The mechanics. The load, the uncoil, the braking mechanism. The why behind a stroke holding up under pressure or falling apart.

I was lucky enough to coach a player who reached a final on technique alone. That stuck with me. Since then I have looked over and rebuilt the technique of more than 200 players, including my own.

I am not here to overhaul your game in a week. I find the fault that matters, fix one thing, and let it settle. Then we move to the next. That is the whole method.

Who I work with

Players I've rebuilt.

Different starting points. Same approach.

Marcel Serve rebuild

A highly ranked junior who burned out and did not touch a racket for four years through college. She came to me to join a high level adult league and rebuild a serve that would not collapse into double faults the moment she got nervous.

The second serve mattered most. A booming ace in a warmup means nothing if the arm tightens on break point. We rebuilt the tossing motion from scratch to kill the tension. Watching her close out games with confidence again has been the whole point.

Sam Pressure proof technique

Played a lot growing up, then stepped away for a decade to focus on his career. He came back wanting competitive matches again, and a technique that holds up when the score gets tight.

That last part mattered most. A stroke that looks beautiful in practice but breaks down the moment it counts is not fixed yet. We tore down some old habits and rebuilt the swing so it holds under real pressure. Seeing him trust it again has been the work.

Dan Forehand overhaul

Scraped by in division three on baseline grinding alone, then quit, frustrated by his own plateau. He came to me to overhaul his forehand so he could dictate points against faster, younger players.

Generating heavy pace under duress mattered most. A penetrating ball off a perfect sitter means nothing if you drop short in a third set tiebreak. We fixed his weight transfer and hip rotation and turned a retriever into a ball striker.

Tom Backhand under pace

Played casual weekends for years, then got tired of being stuck in the middle of the club ladder. He came to me to break into the advanced flights and build a backhand that does not fold when the rallies speed up.

Handling pace mattered most. A clean backhand in a friendly warmup means nothing if the footwork goes lazy and you chunk it into the net on a heavy ball. We shortened his backswing and drilled an early unit turn so he holds the baseline instead of getting pushed back.
